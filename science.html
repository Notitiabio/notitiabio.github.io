What is the gut microbiome?

The gut microbiome, or sometimes referred to as the gut microbiota, is the total complement of microorganisms living inside the human gastrointestinal tract. The complexity and density of bacteria in our gut is such that it can be called our microbial Amazon Jungle. Within the 2kg (4.4 lbs.) of microbes that each of us carries in our guts, there are at least 1000 species of bacteria that produce various metabolites and large molecules, which can interact with our body daily. This molecular crosstalk between the gut microbiome and the human body is the key to how these microbes can affect our health.

    • One example of molecular crosstalk between the gut microbiome and the human body is the production of short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) through bacterial fermentation of dietary fibers. The human host diet provides dietary fibers as energy sources to support bacterial growth, and in return, the bacteria generate SCFAs. The SCFAs are beneficial to the human body in that they provide energy substrate to colonocytes, mitigate inflammation, and regulate satiety. The deficiency of SCFA production has been associated with many diseases, such as type 2 diabetes and colon cancer.

    The Foundation Guild™

    “The Foundation Guild™ plays a fundamental role in structuring a healthy gut microiome and keeping it stable”

    -Dr. Liping Zhao, Co-founder of Notitia Biotechnologies
    Dr. Liping Zhao, the co-founder of Notitia Biotechnologies, discovered in 2018 that just as tall-trees are the foundation species of the rainforest, a select group of gut bacteria works as the foundation species of the gut ecosystem. They play a fundamental role in structuring a healthy gut microbiome and keeping it stable. This group of bacteria is called the “Foundation Guild.”

    The Foundation Guild suppresses disease-causing gut bacteria by acidifying the gut environment, producing anti-microbial compounds, and occupying available ecological niches. When the Foundation Guild diminishes below a critical level, the gut ecosystem starts to collapse, and different pathogens can overgrow and take over the microbiome, consequently inducing or aggravating various diseases ranging from chronic diseases such as diabetes and colon cancer to complications of influenza infection.
    In a clinical trial published in the journal Science, Dr. Zhao’s team tested the Foundation Guild concept on type 2 diabetic (T2D) patients. The team formulated a specific diet, rich in nutrients to support the Foundation Guild bacteria. The diet promoted these essential gut bacteria (circles in the left of the image) and alleviated T2D significantly by suppressing the pathogen bacteria (diamonds in the right of the image). Patients in the treatment group had an average of 20% decrease in HbA1c level, and more than 80% of the patients achieved adequate glycemic control (A1C less than 7% for T2D patients) by the end of the 3-month trial. In two other clinical studies, the Zhao team was able to alleviate childhood genetic obesity and adult metabolic syndrome with the same principle. Patients in the trials lost 10-20% of their initial body weight over 3 months and improved fasting glucose level, lipid profile, and fatty liver, etc.

    These studies, along with many other similar clinical and animal trials from Dr. Zhao’s lab, provide robust evidence to support the importance of the Foundation Guild in structuring a healthier gut microbiome and contributing to human health.
